Laryngeal mask vs. laryngeal tube trial in paediatric patients (LaMaTuPe): a single-blinded, open-label, randomised-controlled trial

Abstract

Background 

In hypoxemic children with difficult airway, or for minor elective procedures, the use of a supraglottic airway device may be preferred to endotracheal intubation, whether with a laryngeal mask or laryngeal tube. Second-generation laryngeal masks may offer a better safety profile. Whether they should be preferred to laryngeal tubes is unknown. This study aimed to compare the efficacy and safety of second-generation laryngeal masks and laryngeal tubes in children.

Methods 

This randomised-controlled trial was conducted in a single university hospital in children <18 years undergoing elective anaesthesia in urology, minor paediatric surgery and gynaecology. Patients were 1 : 1 randomised to the laryngeal mask or laryngeal tube group. Children were allocated a second-generation laryngeal tube or a second-generation laryngeal mask as the primary airway device. The primary endpoint was insertion time. Secondary endpoints included first-attempt success, overall success and complications, which included hypoxia (SpO2 < 90%), laryngospasm, bronchospasm, aspiration and bleeding.

Results 

In total, 135 patients were randomised, with 61 allocated to the laryngeal tube and 74 to the laryngeal mask group, with a median age of 5.4 and 4.9 years, respectively. Median insertion time was significantly longer in the laryngeal tube group (37 vs. 31 s; difference of medians: 6.0 s; 95% confidence interval: 0.0–13.0). The laryngeal tube had a significantly lower first-attempt (41.0%) and overall success rate (45.9%) than the laryngeal mask (90.5% and 97.3%, respectively). Those allocated to the laryngeal tube group had a higher ratio of complications (27.8%) compared to the laryngeal mask group (2.7%).

Conclusion 

This randomised-controlled trial reported that in children undergoing elective anaesthesia, the use of a laryngeal tube was associated with a longer insertion time.

Introduction

Supraglottic airway devices (SADs) have emerged as vital tools, as they can be rapidly inserted. International guidelines endorse SADs as rescue devices for difficult airways, though they do not specify device types [1–4]. In children undergoing minor elective procedures, the use of a SAD can be an alternative to endotracheal intubation, whether with a laryngeal mask or laryngeal tube [5].

Prior studies showed inconclusive results for the efficacy and safety of different SAD types. Comparisons between laryngeal mask and laryngeal tube have been limited to first-generation devices [6–8] and often exclude children ≤10 kg, generating a knowledge gap for this vulnerable cohort [6–9]. In-hospital laryngeal masks are most commonly used, while laryngeal tubes are predominantly used for paediatric out-of-hospital cardiac arrest in Germany, as observational data shows [10]. The relative benefit or harm between different types of SAD remains undetermined. Airway management studies in adults typically use first-attempt success as the primary endpoint [11,12].

Second-generation SADs have a separate drainage channel for a gastric tube compared to first-generation SADs. This is irrespective of the specific device and includes laryngeal tube and laryngeal mask.

This study aimed to compare the effectiveness and safety of a second-generation laryngeal tube and a second-generation laryngeal mask in children undergoing general anaesthesia for elective procedures [6,8,10].

Methods

The study was approved by the Ethics Committee of the Medical Faculty Heidelberg, Germany (S-929/2019) and registered in the German Clinical Trials Register (DRKS00021037). Reporting follows the Consolidated Standards of Reporting Trials guidelines (Supplementary Text S1, Supplemental digital content 1, https://links.lww.com/EJEM/A461).

Study design

This randomised-controlled interventional trial was conducted at a university hospital in Germany, which performed 3729 paediatric general anaesthesias in 2023. In Germany, prehospital emergency physicians are predominantly anaesthesiologists due to the nonexistence of a dedicated emergency medicine speciality [13].

Inclusion and exclusion criteria

Children aged <18 years scheduled for surgery were screened. Included were all children with obtained consent, an American Society of Anesthesiologists (ASA) status ≤3 and a planned surgery time of ≤4 h. Postrandomisation exclusions were made for ethical reasons either if consent was withdrawn, the preferred type of anaesthesia changed or if neuromuscular blocking agents (NMBA) were used during induction or surgery, which would confound the study outcome and could not be controlled for (Supplementary Table 1, Supplemental digital content 1, https://links.lww.com/EJEM/A461). NMBA is an emergency measure that is not routinely carried out in our institution for patients receiving an SAD. Including these patients would skew the study cohort.

According to local regulations, children ≥7 years of age had to provide their assent, in addition to the written informed consent from their caregivers. For children aged <7 years, only the caregiver had to provide written informed consent. Anaesthesiologists were asked to provide written informed consent. Internal data on the number of laryngeal mask applications in children ≤6 years before the study date were sought electronically.

Randomisation and crossover

Patients were randomly assigned to either the laryngeal tube or laryngeal mask group using a web-based tool (www.randomization.com) with block sizes of six children per age group and a 1 : 1 allocation ratio.

Anaesthesiologists enrolled participants during the preoperative anaesthesia clearance without knowledge of the randomisation sequence. After obtaining consent and setting the surgical date, the patient was allocated to the respective trial group. One study team member was present during the induction of anaesthesia and observed the study execution. The treating physician was unaware of the group assignment until the patient arrived at the induction room. Patients and caregivers were blinded to randomisation. As outcome assessors and the person performing the statistical analysis were present during the study execution, further data masking was deferred.

For safety reasons, the treating anaesthesiologist could crossover to a different airway strategy as the allocated airway device failed.

Procedures

A second-generation laryngeal tube (LTS-D, VBM Medical, Sulz am Neckar, Germany) was used as the primary SAD for children in the laryngeal tube group. This product is identical to the Ambu King LTS-D (Supplementary Figure 1, Supplemental digital content 1, https://links.lww.com/EJEM/A461), the most-used SAD in the USA [14]. The second-generation laryngeal tube has a 60-degree curvature, as opposed to 45 degrees, with the idea to better adjust to the pharyngeal anatomic shape. Additionally, thinner cuffs and a conical ramp make the laryngeal tube slimmer [15].

A second-generation laryngeal mask (AuraGain, Ambu GmbH, Bad Nauheim, Germany) was used in patients randomised to the laryngeal mask group. The respective sizes were determined based on the manufacturer’s recommendations (Supplementary Table 2 and Supplementary Figures 2 and 3, Supplemental digital content 1, https://links.lww.com/EJEM/A461). Patients were fasted according to international guidelines and received 0.5 mg kg−1 midazolam orally 1 hour before the induction. Anaesthesia was guided by national guideline recommendations, typically using 3–5 mg kg−1 propofol intravenous and 20 µg kg−1 alfentanil intravenous or 0.33 µg kg−1 min−1 remifentanil intravenous. Inhalational induction of anaesthesia using sevoflurane with 8% volume and a flow of 8 l min−1 was used in children in whom the placement of intravenous access was distressing or painful. Anaesthesia was maintained with sevoflurane in oxygen and air or via propofol.

Airway leakage pressure was determined by closing the expiratory valve with a peak pressure of 35 mmHg and a gas flow of 3 l min−1. The airway pressure curve was observed, and the maximum airway pressure was recorded [6].

A brief introduction to using the laryngeal tube was given. Specific training on laryngeal tubes for anaesthesiologists was deferred to enhance its generalisability for prehospital emergency medicine in Germany.

A follow-up assessment was conducted in the recovery room before ward discharge, evaluating pain with a numeric rating scale (0: no pain; 10: severe pain), questions for sore throat and swallowing difficulties [16].

For children below 4 years of age, the child discomfort and pain scale were employed (Supplementary Table 3, Supplemental digital content 1, https://links.lww.com/EJEM/A461) [17].

Complications were recorded during induction and extubation by the study personnel. Any perioperative complications were recorded by the treating anaesthesiologist (Supplementary Table 4, Supplemental digital content 1, https://links.lww.com/EJEM/A461).

Outcomes

The primary outcome was the insertion time, defined as the time after the facemask was removed until SAD was inserted and a persistent etCO2 trace was observed or until declared SAD failure [11]. In cases of multiple attempts, attempts were timed separately and summed. Insertion times after crossover were excluded in the intention-to-treat (ITT) analysis. There was no predefined maximal number of attempts.

Main secondary endpoints included first-attempt success, defined as successful placement on the first try without repositioning or removal, and overall success [18–20].

The full list of endpoints is presented in Supplementary Table 4, Supplemental digital content 1, https://links.lww.com/EJEM/A461.

Statistical analysis

Based on available studies, a mean difference of 3.9 s for the insertion time in favour of the laryngeal tube was reported [6]. Sample size calculation was based on a power of 80% and a two-sided significance level of 5% for the whole cohort. This led to a calculated sample size of 134 patients (Supplementary Text S2, Supplemental digital content 1, https://links.lww.com/EJEM/A461). Data were collected using Microsoft Excel (Microsoft Deutschland GmbH, Unterschleißheim, Germany). Analyses were performed using SPSS (Statistical Product and Services Solutions, Version 29, SPSS Inc., Chicago, Illinois). Prism (GraphPad Prism, Version 9) visualised primary and selected secondary endpoints.

An ITT analysis was performed for the primary and selected secondary outcomes. Airway leakage pressure and volume were analysed on a per-protocol basis.

A two-sided level of significance of 5% was considered statistically significant. Demographic and baseline clinical characteristics were presented as mean with SD for parametric or median with 25% and 75% quartiles [interquartile range (IQR)] for nonparametric continuous data and as counts and percentages for categorical parameters. Four age groups were predefined: (1) 0 to <3 years, (2) ≥ 3 to <7 years, (3) ≥7 to <12 years and (4) ≥12 to <18 years.

The treating anaesthesiologist determined ASA status. Mallampati score was not assessed, due to recruitment during COVID-19 pandemic.

Patient or caregiver self-reported data on gender allowed indication whether the person identified as female, male, diverse or nonbinary.

Differences in the time until success or failure between the two groups were assessed using the Mann–Whitney U test. The effect size for the primary outcome was calculated as the differences between pseudo medians with nonparametric 95% confidence interval (CI) using the Hodges–Lehmann method. Overall and first-attempt success were analysed using the chi-squared test. Further secondary endpoints were analysed using the chi-squared test for categorical variables and the t-test with 95% CI for continuous variables. Missing data were not imputed.

Due to the high number of crossovers, demographic parameters are presented for ITT and per-protocol analysis.

Results

Between 25 January 2021 and 14 February 2023, 229 children were assessed for eligibility, with 196 undergoing randomisations. Of those, 61 and 74 children were analysed in the laryngeal tube and laryngeal mask group, respectively (Fig. 1).

The mean age was 5.4 (SD 4.7) and 4.9 (SD 4.1) years in the laryngeal tube and laryngeal mask groups, respectively. About 36% of the children were in the 0–2 age group. Baseline characteristics were well balanced between both ITT groups (Table 1). Participating anaesthesiologists had a mean experience of 5.7 (SD 7.3) and 5.1 (SD 4.9) years. The median duration of anaesthesia in the laryngeal tube and laryngeal mask groups was 32 (IQR: 12.0–54.0) minutes and 35 (IQR: 20–61.75) minutes, respectively. The most frequent surgical procedures across both groups were metal removal (n = 30), circumcision (n = 24) and DJ exchange or removal (n = 15). The complete list of surgical procedures is presented in Supplementary Table 5, Supplemental digital content 1, https://links.lww.com/EJEM/A461.

Primary endpoint: insertion time

The median time to insertion was significantly longer in the laryngeal tube group: 37.0 s (IQR: 26.0–60.0) vs. 31.0 s (IQR: 25.0–39.0) laryngeal mask [difference of medians 6.0 s (95% CI: 0.0–13.0), P = 0.031].

Secondary endpoints

In this study, laryngeal tube achieved a first-attempt and overall success rate of 41.0% and 45.9%, respectively. The laryngeal mask achieved a first-attempt and overall success rate of 90.5% and 97.3%, respectively (Table 2). This resulted in an overall high rate of crossovers from laryngeal tube to laryngeal mask (54%, n = 33), with the highest rates in the age groups 0–2 (15/22; 68%) and 3–6 years (10/17; 59%). These differences were statistically significant compared to the laryngeal mask group within the same age group (Table 3 and Supplementary Figure 4, Supplemental digital content 1, https://links.lww.com/EJEM/A461).

In the laryngeal tube group, 17 complications were reported compared to two in the laryngeal mask group. Hypoxia was solely reported in the laryngeal tube group (Table 3).

Exploratory analyses

In the overall per-protocol analysis, laryngeal tube achieved higher airway leakage pressures with 30.4 vs. 24.5 cmH20 (P < 0.001), respectively. Accordingly, leakage volume per minute was lower in the laryngeal tube group compared to the laryngeal mask group (1.3% vs. 2.8%; P = 0.068) without reaching statistical significance (Table 3). Despite the statistical significance, the higher airway leakage pressure is considered clinically insignificant, as 20.0 cmH20 should not be exceeded when a SAD is used.

There was no statistical difference in insertion time in children with ≤10 kg body weight (Supplementary Tables 6 and 7, Supplemental digital content 1, https://links.lww.com/EJEM/A461).

As-treated analysis

The insertion times between a successful laryngeal tube and laryngeal mask were similar (30.5 vs. 31.0 s) (Supplementary Figure 5 and Supplementary Table 8, Supplemental digital content 1, https://links.lww.com/EJEM/A461).

In the as-treated laryngeal mask analysis, the median insertion time in crossed applications (n = 28) was 28.0 s (IQR: 21.25–32.0), compared to the initial assigned laryngeal mask group [−3.0 s (95% CI: −9.0 to −1.0); P = 0.009], indicating a patent airway despite previous failed laryngeal tube attempts.

Discussion

Our trial included 135 children and found a longer insertion time in the laryngeal tube group compared to the laryngeal mask group. The difference in medians between the two groups was 6 s (95% CI: 0.0–13.0), achieving statistical significance. The laryngeal tube group had lower first-attempt and overall success rates and a higher desaturation rate (10% vs. 0%) during induction, suggesting the clinical significance of the laryngeal mask.

A subgroup analysis of children ≤10 kg showed similar success rates with both first-attempt success and overall success of 27%, which is comparatively low [22]. For the laryngeal tube group, the findings imply that sufficient bag-mask ventilation has been abandoned for an uncertain airway device.

Despite higher complication rates in the laryngeal tube group, neither patient required prolonged observation or unplanned ICU admission.

This is the largest randomised-controlled trial (RCT) assessing two different second-generation SAD in children available. In other RCTs, children with ≤10 kg bodyweight were either excluded [6,9] or underrepresented [7]. A case series reported successful laryngeal tube placement in 10 children below 6 months without a comparator [23].

Three study team members conducted a rigorous endpoint assessment to limit interobserver variability. This study involved 46 anaesthesiologists with an average experience of 5 years. Although SADs are regularly used, laryngeal tubes are not included in the national training curriculum. Some providers might have used laryngeal tubes in adults prehospital, but this was not evaluated. Prior experience defined as years in anaesthesia, prior count of laryngeal mask inserted in children <6 years or board-certified-specialist-status had no significant effect on the primary outcome. The cutoff of <6 years was made after seeing many failed insertions in this group. This study involved more providers than an RCT where only one consultant performed the intervention [7], enhancing generalisability and applicability to junior anaesthesiologists. Since airway management in children is routinely conducted by anaesthesiologists and paediatricians [24], we expect a similar success rate for the used devices could be observed in less-trained personnel.

The insertion time reported here is longer than in a previous RCT, with 37 s for the laryngeal tube group (vs. 19.2 s) and 31 s for the laryngeal mask group (vs. 23.1 s) [6].

A paediatric manikin study showed a 100% success rate using a second-generation laryngeal tube in physiological conditions, tongue oedema or cervical immobilisation [22]. In contrast, one RCT reported a 27% failure rate for laryngeal tube (4/15) [7], whereas two other studies showed a 100% overall success rate [6,9]. This study had an overall failure rate for the laryngeal tube group of 54% (33/61).

The induction of anaesthesia is comparable to the study by Genzwuerker [6]. Kaya et al. routinely used neuromuscular blocking (NMB) drugs [9], a predefined exclusion criterion in this study. Anaesthesia was induced with sevoflurane via a facemask, and pain control was achieved through nerve blocks in the study by Bortone et al. [7]. The use of NMB drugs or an insertion during spontaneous airway maintenance may facilitate the use of any SAD. Both muscle relaxation and spontaneous breathing create conditions that simplify the technical aspects of SAD placement [25]. Muscle relaxation eliminates resistance from tense muscles, while spontaneous breathing ensures continuous airway patency. The use of NMBs or spontaneous breathing can mitigate common complications associated with SAD placement, such as airway trauma or incorrect positioning.

One of this study’s intentions was to evaluate laryngeal tube efficacy and safety in children weighing ≤10 kg. This cohort was excluded in previous analyses. This group, however, has the highest incidence of paediatric out-of-hospital cardiac arrest [10]. Success rates for laryngeal mask and laryngeal tube were comparable to those of the overall cohort, without differences in insertion time. The small sample size within the respective subgroups weakens the statistical finding. Our findings imply that laryngeal mask should be the preferred SAD device in children weighing ≤10 kg. In contrast, a case series by Scheller et al. reported a successful insertion in 10 children under 6 months [26]. The retrospective nature and a missing control group limit the report. Future studies should assess the learning curve of different SAD in children, with participants undergoing standardised training. This should be evaluated prospectively in a multicentre design to increase generalisability and understand the reason behind the failure of SAD placement. A patient-centred outcome such as ‘hypoxia’ can be used in a composite primary outcome to assess device handling and clinical factors.

Limitations

Blinding the treating anaesthesia team was not feasible, so the allocated airway device was declared shortly before patient’s arrival.

Per institutional standards, laryngeal masks and endotracheal tubes were always available. All participants were trained with the laryngeal mask used herein in adult patients, reflected in the laryngeal mask group’s high success rate and quicker insertion time. The availability of a laryngeal mask and the lack of a predefined number of insertion attempts likely influenced earlier decision to crossover, as reflected by the number of insertion attempts in the laryngeal tube group. The total induction time was not recorded in this study due to different approaches by the conducting anaesthesiologist. Adding this time to a specific endpoint would introduce a bias that cannot be controlled. Emergency cases with nonfastened patients were excluded, as these would not be managed with a SAD in our institution.

Sevoflurane is not available in the emergency medicine setting and propofol should only be used with utmost caution. Other induction drugs, such as midazolam or ketamine, could have a different effect on airway management.

Participating anaesthesiologists may have been prone to the Hawthorne effect by the presence of a study member [27].

This study observed an unexpectedly high dropout rate. Although there is no statistical difference in baseline characteristics, this could introduce selection bias after randomisation.

The use of ‘face mask removal’ as T0 for the calculation of the primary endpoint may be subject to bias compared to other time stamps, such as induction or apnoea. Children were bag-mask ventilated until the SAD was inserted. In cases of multiple attempts, bag-mask ventilation was resumed at the discretion of the treating anaesthesiologist.

Finally, the overall high failure rate and crossover limit the validity of these results.

Conclusion

This RCT reported that in children undergoing elective anaesthesia, the use of laryngeal tubes was associated with a prolonged insertion time and lower rates of the first-attempt and overall success. The high insertion failure rate and small sample size, however, may limit the validity of these results [10,14].
